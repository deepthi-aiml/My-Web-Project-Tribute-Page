Marie Curie

Pioneer of Radioactivity and First Woman to Win a Nobel Prize

The Life of a Scientific Trailblazer

1867

Maria Salomea Skłodowska is born in Warsaw, Poland, then part of the Russian Empire.

1891

Moves to Paris to study at the Sorbonne, where she would earn degrees in Physics and Mathematical Sciences.

1895

Marries Pierre Curie, beginning one of science's most famous partnerships.

1898

Discovers the elements polonium (named after her native Poland) and radium.

1903

Becomes the first woman to win a Nobel Prize, sharing the Physics prize with Pierre Curie and Henri Becquerel for their work on radioactivity.

1906

After Pierre's tragic death, she takes over his professorship at the Sorbonne, becoming the first woman to teach there.

1911

Wins her second Nobel Prize, this time in Chemistry, for the discovery of radium and polonium, making her the first person to win Nobel Prizes in two different sciences.

1914-1918

Develops mobile X-ray units during World War I, known as "Little Curies," to help treat wounded soldiers.

1934

Dies from aplastic anemia, likely caused by prolonged exposure to radiation during her research.

Major Achievements

Pioneering Radioactivity Research

Coined the term "radioactivity" and developed the theory of radioactivity

Discovery of Elements

Discovered polonium and radium, expanding the periodic table

Medical Applications

Pioneered the use of radiation in medicine, particularly for cancer treatment

Double Nobel Laureate

First person to win Nobel Prizes in two different scientific fields

"Nothing in life is to be feared, it is only to be understood. Now is the time to understand more, so that we may fear less."

- Marie Curie

Lasting Legacy

Marie Curie's groundbreaking work laid the foundation for modern nuclear physics and cancer treatments. She broke barriers for women in science and inspired generations of researchers. Her notebooks remain so radioactive that they are stored in lead-lined boxes, a testament to her dedication and the dangers she faced in pursuit of knowledge.

The Curie family legacy continued with her daughter Irène Joliot-Curie, who also won a Nobel Prize in Chemistry in 1935.
